## PAGINE INTERNE

**[Testo colonna sinistra]**

Driven by the desire to recover and share the prestigious Past of his Family by bringing it back to life and making it known in the near Future — assisted and supported by a team of valid professionals in the sector — in 2025 Annibale Bianco gave life to the Ciuppa Tasca Farm Museum, which in the demo-ethno-anthropological field immediately stands out as a cultural reference point for Sicily and beyond. The Museum was born with a clear connotation represented by its logo, consisting of an olive-fingerprint supported by the typical merlons of the Farm's architecture, and by the claim IDENTITY. INNOVATION. ENCOUNTERS.

A bold synthesis between industrial archaeology and a digital Ecomuseum, this site aims to pay homage to a Community and a Territory that specifically find themselves in this precise place and in this determined period but which in reality, with various declinations, constitute the common History of the Mediterranean over the last millennia. Here the cycle of olive oil production reclaims the fundamental role it has always played in our daily lives: from mythology to religion, from archaeology to art, from craftsmanship to commerce, from agronomy to lighting, from sports to nutrition, from medicine to cosmetics.

This Museum is not a closed place disconnected from the Community, but rather a permanent institution at the service of Society, accessible and inclusive for all, which researches, collects, preserves, interprets and exhibits cultural heritage, both material and immaterial, offering diversified experiences for education, pleasure, reflection, and the sharing of knowledge. In addition to the Reception and the actual Exhibition Hall, this complex houses a Drying Laboratory, a Conference Room, an open central Courtyard, and several hectares of land, mainly cultivated with olive and pomegranate trees, where various wild herbs also grow.

We are located on "Cozzo Parrino" (about 30 meters above sea level) in Contrada Baroni within the territory of Noto, just a few kilometers from Vendicari, Marzamemi, and Pachino.

The land is mostly flat or gently sloping, presenting particular outcrops of yellow-white biocalcarenites dating back to the Upper Middle Pleistocene (Quaternary), which also contain interesting fossil remains. In its superficial part, it is characterized by an alternation of silt, clay, and sand which, together with the presence of organic substances and mineral components, as well as the favorable exposure to winds and sunlight, make the soil fertile and flourishing for the species, both spontaneous and cultivated, that grow and proliferate here.

**[Testo colonna centrale / destra]**

These spaces host book presentations, theatrical pieces, conventions and round tables, training courses, show cooking, laboratories and workshops, sensory and emotional journeys, as well as playful and recreational activities, always with one eye on the Past and one on the Future. Among the most requested and attended activities by visitors are: the extra virgin olive oil tasting course, the sensory journey in the olive grove, the recognition and collection of wild herbs, the preparation of typical Sicilian foods with locally sourced products, the "treasure hunt" to discover ancient objects linked to the past peasant civilization, and the workshop on how to make soaps using oil flavored with various local essences (prickly pear, broom, orange blossom, pomegranate, carob, almond).

Overlooking and harmonizing this wonderful landscape is a typical Sicilian farmhouse from the early 1900s, built like a quadrilateral fortress around a welcoming central courtyard, which for centuries has been the beating heart of the family's production activities centered on Olive Growing and which today is the seat of the Museum of Oil.

In the Ciuppa Tasca Farm, starting from the 1930s, there has been an important mechanized oil mill line used until the 1980s, which today — after a meticulous recovery work — constitutes an admirable example of Industrial Archaeology in the oil sector.

The main restoration work was conducted on the historic building which in recent decades had been subject to the gradual deterioration of building materials, undergoing a progressive process of degradation and alteration that had also compromised the relationship with the surrounding spaces.

In the past, these places were part of a larger fiefdom that from the 1700s belonged to the Mastrogiovanni Tasca family, one of the most noble houses in Sicily, which counts among its illustrious descendants the poet Lucio Piccolo and the writer Giuseppe Tomasi di Lampedusa, author of The Leopard.

## PAGINE ESTERNE

**[Testo descrittivo frantoio]**

In the oil mill and surrounding areas, careful recovery work was carried out on: the load-bearing structure of the building made of thick stone masonry, the pitched roof with wooden beams, boarding, and traditional Sicilian roof tiles, and the flooring, partly consisting of natural stone slabs and partly cemented.

The elements making up the entire oil mill line also showed deterioration and damage due to wear, abandonment, and weathering. These too have been the subject of a meticulous conservative and functional recovery of the original mechanical components, carried out with non-invasive materials and techniques compatible with their original production era. These interventions have made it possible to preserve the historical, aesthetic, and even philological integrity of the oil mill, making it safe, pleasant, and readable for educational and informative purposes. Due to structural requirements, the electrical panels and the opposed-piston diesel engine, built by Muzzi under Junkers license, are located in the adjacent "Sala Marina", while in the "Sala Francesca" one can still admire in their original position — and function: an FMI 2/140 millstone crusher with lower mechanical drive and fixed and idler pulleys, an "Italia" high and low pressure hydraulic pump with automatic detachment, a Separator, a PP175 hydraulic press made of seamless forged special steel suitable for processing with fiscoli (filtering mats), a D155 Superpress for flat-trolley use, and a PA175/3 three-column press.

The collection is completed by various artifacts related in various ways to the world of oil: starting from the tools for working the land and managing the olive trees, to objects that testify to the various common uses of extra virgin olive oil that characterize our daily lives, to which too often we do not pay the right attention and do not grant the deserved recognition.
